RKCT 4.4 – Core Model of the Transition Sequence

Version 4.4 presents the most refined formulation of the Recursive Knowledge Cycle Theory (RKCT). It describes a generalized transition sequence through which knowledge systems reorganize themselves, resolve accumulated tensions, and generate new levels of structure. The model consists of six phases that together form a recursive cycle.

1. Initial Stabilization

In the stabilization phase, a system maintains a coherent and reproducible structure. Patterns are reinforced through repetition, feedback, and functional integration. Variability is low, and optimization occurs within established boundaries.

2. Accumulation of Tensions

Internal or external pressures increase as environmental conditions shift, inconsistencies grow, or resource constraints intensify. The stabilized structure becomes increasingly strained and less capable of accommodating rising complexity.

3. Structural Breakdown

When accumulated tensions exceed the system’s capacity for stabilization, structural coherence collapses. Constraints weaken, variability increases, and the system enters a phase of instability. Formerly reliable patterns become unpredictable.

4. Exploratory Expansion

The system generates a wide range of structural or conceptual variants. Novel combinations, mutations, or reorganizations emerge. Most variants remain unstable, but some introduce new pathways for development and potential stabilization.

5. Re‑Consolidation

Successful variants are stabilized and integrated into a new structural baseline. The system regains coherence at a higher level of organization. New constraints emerge, enabling reliable reproduction of the new structure.

6. Recursive Embedding

The newly consolidated structure becomes embedded within a broader recursive cycle. Each completed transition forms the foundation for subsequent cycles, enabling long‑term evolutionary development across biological, cultural, technological, and artificial systems.
